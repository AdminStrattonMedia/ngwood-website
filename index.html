Wood Cutting Methods

How the Log is
Cut Makes All the Difference

The angle at which a log is sliced determines the grain pattern, figure, and stability of the veneer. Understanding the cut helps you choose the right look for your project.

Log Cross-Section — Cut Comparison
Most Common
Flat Cut
Also: Plain Sliced

Cut parallel to the center of the log. Produces a cathedral arch or "flame" grain pattern. The most widely used cut — natural, warm, and familiar.

Cathedral arch grain figure
Best yield from log
Traditional & versatile
Quarter Cut
Also: Quarter Sawn

Log is quartered first, then sliced perpendicular to growth rings. Produces tight straight grain with visible ray fleck — a prized, premium look especially in oak.

Straight grain + ray fleck
More dimensionally stable
Premium cabinetry & furniture
Rift Cut
Also: Rift Sawn

Cut at a 15° angle from the quartered position. Produces the most uniform, perfectly straight grain — no fleck, no figure. Clean and modern.

Perfectly uniform grain lines
No ray fleck or figure
Preferred for modern design
Rotary Cut
Also: Peeled / Lathe Cut

The entire log is spun on a lathe and peeled in a continuous sheet. Produces a bold, wide, irregular grain pattern. Most common in plywood cores.

Wide, bold grain pattern
Highest yield per log
Common in plywood & cores

Not sure which cut is right for your project? Call us — we'll help you choose.
